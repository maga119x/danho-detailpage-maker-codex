# Conversion Desire Architecture

Use this when copy is natural but still does not create purchase desire. This is product-category agnostic. Do not turn examples or validation artifacts into product-specific rules.

## Core Principle

Natural Korean is only the baseline. A detail page must make the buyer understand:

1. who this is for
2. what frustrating before-state they are leaving
3. what better after-state they get
4. why the product mechanism makes that change believable
5. why the value feels confident, not apologetic
6. what proof or visual demonstration supports the claim
7. what to understand before using the store purchase area

If a section is grammatically clean but does not move one of these decisions, revise it.

These are strategy questions only. Do not translate this vocabulary into visible Korean copy. After filling this architecture, rewrite it as Korean buyer/seller verbatims before drafting display copy.

## Target Desire Lock

Define the buyer by situation and desire, not only demographics.

| Field | Good answer |
|---|---|
| buyer situation | The repeated moment where the buyer feels friction. |
| buyer desire | Relief, control, speed, pride, safety, competence, order, comfort, or confidence after purchase. |
| desired self-image | The buyer's wanted identity in the category: prepared, capable, tasteful, careful, efficient, relaxed, etc. |
| purchase trigger | The moment that makes "I should buy this now" feel reasonable. |

Avoid shallow identity copy. Do not stereotype gender, age, job, or lifestyle. Use the buyer's real desired outcome.

## Customer Verbatim Bridge

After strategy, write the buyer's thought in everyday Korean.

| Strategy | Verbatim bridge |
|---|---|
| desired self-image | `이 정도면 처음 시작하기 괜찮겠다` |
| value stack | `칼만 오는 게 아니라 같이 필요한 것도 있네` |
| reduced friction | `따로 찾을 일이 줄겠네` |
| mechanism | `아, 이렇게 쓰면 편하겠구나` |

Only the verbatim bridge may become visible copy. Strategy labels must stay in planning notes.

## Before/After Rule

At minimum, hero, problem, core solution, key benefit, value, and final product/result closing should carry a before/after shift.

- Before: what currently feels annoying, slow, uncertain, embarrassing, risky, wasteful, or incomplete.
- External cause: the current tool, habit, setup, product gap, environment, or missing information. Do not blame the buyer's ability.
- Product mechanism: the specific feature, composition, design, service, or guide that changes the situation.
- After: the buyer's improved moment in concrete language.

Weak: `사용이 편리합니다`
Strong: `매번 다시 확인하던 일을 줄여, 바로 다음 행동으로 넘어갈 수 있어요`

## Value Framing Without Direct Price

Do not make price feel like a problem before the buyer raises it. Avoid defensive frames such as `비싼 게 아니라` or repeated explanations that imply apology.

Because direct numeric prices are not allowed in visible detail-page copy, justify value through:

- value stack: what is included together
- avoided extra purchase: what the buyer does not need to search for separately
- reduced hassle: fewer decisions, fewer steps, fewer accessories to buy later
- durability or repeat-use logic, when supported
- comparison with the common alternative, without attacking competitors
- mutable benefit facts: keep them in editable factual/options sections only; do not use the final closing for price, option, benefit-check, or purchase-action prompts

Fail if the value section only says where to check price. It must explain why the offer is worth checking.
Also fail if visible copy names the sales channel (`스마트스토어`, `쿠팡`, `자사몰`, `판매 채널`) or repeatedly says `구매 페이지에서 확인`; the buyer is already on the selling page.

## Confidence And Caveat Placement

Selling sections should sound confident within verified facts. Caveats and limits should be concentrated in specs, safety/care, compatibility, or FAQ.

- Put repeated `확인해 주세요` statements in the factual section unless the check is a purchase blocker.
- Do not scatter safety disclaimers or uncertainty across benefit sections.
- If a claim is not proven, either soften the claim or mark `NEEDS_PROOF`; do not fill the page with hedging.

## Visual Proof Planning

Conversion often depends on proof images more than wording. Each important claim needs a visual proof role.

| Claim type | Strong visual proof |
|---|---|
| problem | before scene, failed workaround, inconvenient current behavior |
| mechanism | close-up, step sequence, usage demo, component layout |
| before/after | side-by-side comparison, motion/GIF/video direction, measurable result if supplied |
| value stack | all included parts/services/benefits shown as one decision |
| trust | real review, test, certificate, warranty, manufacturing/process proof |
| final aspiration | buyer's better moment after purchase |

Do not let product flatlays replace proof. Product shots are useful, but at least the core problem and core solution should have demonstration-oriented visuals when the category allows it.

## Fail Patterns

- The page repeats features but never states the buyer's desired after-state.
- The copy is polite and accurate but feels timid, apologetic, or noncommittal.
- The value section defends against price instead of building value confidence.
- Purchase readiness is reduced to `check the purchase page`, a named channel, or a final-section action prompt without giving a reason to want the offer.
- Caveats, safety notes, or `check details` language appear in multiple selling sections.
- Visual directions are mostly decorative product shots instead of problem/solution proof.
